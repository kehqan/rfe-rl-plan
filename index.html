01 — Problem framing

What a newsroom actually needs from AI

RFE/RL operates across dozens of services, languages, and editorial cultures. AI doesn't solve all of that — but it can reduce friction in the right places.

01
Research takes too long
Journalists spend hours scanning sources, catching up on background context, or verifying what's already been covered. Time that could go to reporting.
02
Editorial bandwidth is limited
Editors are stretched. Structural checks, consistency reviews, and format guidance often fall through the gaps — not from negligence, but from volume.
03
Multilingual complexity adds friction
Adapting content across language services is time-consuming. Drafting, translating, and localizing for each audience multiplies effort.
04
New tools create resistance
Journalists have seen "solutions" come and go. Adoption fails when tools add steps instead of removing them. Trust is earned, not assumed.

"Transformation that earns adoption moves faster than transformation that demands it — especially in a newsroom."

Core premise — this plan

02 — Core principles

How transformation actually works here

Sustainable digital transformation in a newsroom isn't a single initiative — it's a series of small, validated improvements. These principles shape every decision in this plan.

Human-in-the-loop, always

Every AI output is a draft, a suggestion, or a starting point. Journalists and editors make the final call — always. AI doesn't publish, decide, or verify.

Start small, prove value

No organization-wide rollouts in 90 days. We begin with 2–3 willing journalists, iterate on what works, and scale only what earns its place.

Editorial standards are not negotiable

AI assists with structure, summarization, and drafting — not with fact-checking or truth verification. Editorial judgment stays with the team.

Simple systems over clever ones

If a workflow can't be explained in a sentence, it won't be used. We favor tools that integrate quietly into existing habits over those that demand new ones.

No hype, no promises

We measure what we can actually measure. Adoption rates, time saved, and journalist satisfaction — not "transformation" or "efficiency gains."

Incremental trust-building

Resistance to new tools is rational. We earn adoption by making early experiences low-stakes, reversible, and genuinely useful.

AI = assistant layer Not autonomous agents Not replacing journalists Current tools only Reversible experiments Credibility over impressiveness

03 — Three-layer system

Different stakeholders, different needs

AI assistance looks different at each level of the organization. A single tool for everyone fails everyone.

Layer 1
Production
Journalists · Designers
Research summarization — background context, archive search, source overview; journalist verifies and uses selectively
Story structuring assistance — prompt-based outlines, angle suggestions; journalist owns the editorial direction
Translation drafting support — AI-assisted first draft for multilingual adaptation; human editor refines and localizes
Layer 2
Editorial management
Editors · Team leads
Structure and consistency checks — flag missing elements (lede, attribution, context) in a story draft; not fact-checking
Workflow visibility — simple dashboard showing content in progress, format distribution, and output by service
Content tagging assistant — semi-automated topic and format classification; editor reviews before publishing
Layer 3
Strategic / board
Editorial leadership
Output overview — rolling summary of content volume, format mix, and multilingual reach across services; no operational detail
Efficiency signals — high-level indicators of where AI-assisted workflows are having an effect; not micromanagement data
What's not here: Fully autonomous content pipelines, AI publishing decisions, complex multi-agent systems, or anything that removes a human from the loop. These layers are additive — they sit alongside existing workflows, not replacing them.

04 — 90-day phases

A realistic timeline

Three distinct phases with clear outputs. Each one builds on the last. Nothing moves to production before it's been tested with real users.

Phase one
Discovery & mapping
Days 1–30
  • Interview journalists across 3–4 services
  • Shadow editorial workflow sessions
  • Audit tools already in use
  • Identify top 2–3 high-impact bottlenecks
  • Meet cross-team partners (R&D, Academy, Product)
  • Align with editorial leadership on scope
  • Produce: Discovery report + 90-day sprint plan
Output: understanding, not tools
Phase two
Prototyping & testing
Days 30–60
  • Build 2–3 lightweight AI-assisted workflows
  • Run with 3–5 willing journalists (opt-in only)
  • Weekly feedback sessions, iterate quickly
  • Test research summarization assistant
  • Test story structure prompt templates
  • Test translation drafting support
  • Produce: Feedback report + refined prototypes
Output: tested prototypes
Phase three
Integration (beta)
Days 60–90
  • Turn 2 prototypes into repeatable workflows
  • Write internal playbooks (non-technical)
  • Run onboarding sessions with Academy
  • Deploy simple editorial dashboard
  • Measure early impact (self-reported + observed)
  • 30-day summary report to leadership
  • Produce: Beta workflows + phase 2 proposal
Output: repeatable beta workflows
What "beta" means here: A workflow used voluntarily by a small group, with clear documentation, human oversight at every step, and a feedback channel. Not a full rollout. The 90-day goal is to arrive at 2–3 things that demonstrably work — then scale carefully from there.

05 — Example workflows

What this looks like in practice

Three concrete use cases with step-by-step flows. Each keeps the journalist in control at every stage.

Research summarization
Story structure assistant
Translation drafting
Research summarization assistant
Journalist is covering a fast-moving story. Needs quick background on key actors, previous coverage, and context — without spending 2 hours in archives.
Layer 1 · Phase 2
Step-by-step flow
1
Journalist
Defines the scope of the query
Pastes 3–5 source documents (wire copy, prior RFE/RL pieces, official statements) into the tool, or provides a topic description and time range.
2
AI assistant
Generates a structured background brief
Returns: key actors and their roles, timeline of events, what's already been covered, and 3–4 open questions for further reporting.
AI note: outputs are labeled "draft summary — verify before use." No sources are fabricated; AI works only from provided documents.
3
Journalist
Reviews and selects what to use
Journalist reads the brief, cross-checks any claims they're unsure of, discards what's wrong or irrelevant, and uses the rest as a starting point for reporting.
4
Journalist
Reports and writes normally
The AI-generated brief is a reference document, not copy. All published content is the journalist's own work. The tool saved research time — nothing more.
Story structure assistant
Journalist has reported a story but is stuck on structure. The angle is clear, but the piece needs a better lede, flow, or format decision.
Layer 1 · Phase 2
Step-by-step flow
1
Journalist
Shares the raw material
Pastes their notes, a rough draft, or a set of key facts into the tool. Adds a one-line prompt: "Help me think through structure for a 600-word explainer on X."
2
AI assistant
Proposes 2–3 structural options
Returns: option A (inverted pyramid for breaking news), option B (narrative lede + context block for feature angle), option C (Q&A format if this is an explainer). Each includes a suggested lede sentence.
AI note: these are structural suggestions, not editorial decisions. The journalist decides angle, framing, and what the story is actually about.
3
Journalist
Selects, adapts, or ignores
Picks the structure that fits, rewrites the lede in their own voice, and builds the piece. The AI output is a thinking prompt — not a template to fill in.
4
Editor
Reviews and edits as normal
Editor sees the finished piece — not the AI suggestions. Editorial review is unchanged. The tool is invisible to the final product.
Translation drafting support
A story published in English needs to be adapted for a regional language service. The service editor needs a starting draft to work from, not a finished piece.
Layer 1–2 · Phase 2–3
Step-by-step flow
1
Source desk
Flags story for multilingual adaptation
Editor on the English desk marks the story as "adaptation-ready" and notes which regional services should receive it.
2
AI assistant
Generates a working translation draft
Tool produces a literal draft translation, flagging idioms, cultural references, or phrases that may not translate directly. Notes are inline, not hidden.
AI note: translation is a first draft only. Regional editors are required to review, adapt tone, and ensure cultural accuracy before publication.
3
Regional editor
Reviews, localizes, and rewrites where needed
Regional editor treats the AI draft as a rough starting point. They rewrite sentences that feel unnatural, adjust for local context, and verify any names or references specific to the region.
4
Regional editor
Publishes under their editorial sign-off
The published piece carries the regional team's editorial responsibility. Time saved: the draft removes the blank-page problem, not the editorial judgment.

06 — Collaboration model

Who this role works with, and how

The Creative AI Specialist sits at the intersection of editorial, product, and training. The role only works if it has active relationships across all four areas.

Innovation / R&D
Experimentation partner
  • Co-develop and test new AI tool prototypes
  • Share discovery findings to inform R&D priorities
  • Evaluate new model capabilities before newsroom rollout
  • Bi-weekly sync during phases 2 and 3
Internal Academy
Journalist onboarding
  • Co-design onboarding sessions for new AI workflows
  • Translate playbooks into training-ready formats
  • Identify journalists ready to be early adopters
  • Support ongoing learning as tools evolve
Product / Digital teams
Tool integration
  • Ensure AI tools integrate with existing CMS and workflows
  • Advise on API connections and data flow requirements
  • Co-build the editorial workflow dashboard
  • Monthly alignment on roadmap and technical constraints
Editorial leadership
Trust and alignment
  • Monthly check-in on editorial priorities and concerns
  • Share discovery and prototype outcomes at each phase
  • Surface resistance early — don't wait for it to escalate
  • 30-day summary report at end of phase 3
A note on pace: These relationships don't all need to be at full intensity from day one. Phase 1 is primarily listening. Deep collaboration with Product and Academy begins in phases 2 and 3 — once there's something concrete to build around.

07 — Impact measurement

What we measure, and how

We measure what we can actually observe. No inflated metrics. No claims that can't be substantiated.

Layer 1 — Production metrics
Tool adoption rate
Number of journalists using AI-assisted workflows at least once per week (self-reported log)
Monthly · Phase 3
Research time saved
Self-reported before/after estimates from pilot journalists — "how long did this take before vs now?" Qualitative, not precise
End of Phase 2
Draft usability score
After each AI-assisted draft, journalist rates: "Was this useful?" (1–5 scale) + optional comment. Tracked weekly
Ongoing · Phase 2–3
Layer 2 — Editorial metrics
Editorial check completion rate
Percentage of stories that go through AI-assisted structure check before publication (for participating desks)
Phase 3
Tagging consistency
Compare manual vs AI-assisted tags across a sample of 50 stories — percentage agreement reviewed by editor
Phase 3
Qualitative signals (equally important)
Journalist sentiment
Monthly 5-question survey: Did the tool save time? Did it interfere with your work? Would you use it again?
Monthly
Resistance signals
Track reported complaints, tool abandonment, and friction points. Negative signals are treated as product feedback, not failure
Ongoing
What we don't measure: "AI-generated content percentage" (not meaningful), "efficiency gains" without a baseline (not credible), or anything that pressures journalists to use tools they don't find useful. The goal is adoption that earns itself.

08 — Value proposition

A practical path into transformation

Digital transformation in a newsroom doesn't happen in a single rollout. In 90 days, this plan validates the approach, builds trust with teams, and creates the conditions for sustainable change.

01
A validated starting point
A documented map of where AI can genuinely help, backed by interviews with actual journalists — the evidence base for every decision that follows.
02
2–3 proven AI workflows
Tested with real users, refined through feedback, documented for non-technical staff. Small in scope — but ready to scale because they've earned it.
03
Journalist buy-in, started
Early adopters who've had a genuinely positive experience. A small group that can advocate for transformation from inside the newsroom — the most credible voice.
04
Cross-team alignment built
Active working relationships with R&D, Academy, Product, and Editorial leadership — the organizational infrastructure that makes transformation stick.
05
A credible phase 2 roadmap
Based on real evidence: what worked, what didn't, and what the next 90 days should look like. Transformation that's earned, not assumed.
06
Editorial standards intact
No shortcuts on verification, no autonomous publishing, no promises broken. Sustainable transformation only works if the newsroom's credibility is never at risk.

"Transformation that lasts is built in small steps — validated with the people it's meant to serve, scaled only after it's proven its value, and always aligned with how the newsroom actually works."
